Introduction

College Algebra is a foundational mathematics course that focuses on the study of algebraic concepts, which are essential for higher-level math courses such as calculus, statistics, and linear algebra. It bridges the gap between basic algebra learned in high school and more advanced topics in mathematics. College algebra is not only valuable for students pursuing STEM fields (like science, technology, engineering, and mathematics), but it also has real-world applications in areas like economics, business, and social sciences

Key Concepts

  1. Real Numbers and Their Properties:
    • Real Numbers: These include integers, rational numbers, and irrational numbers. The study of real numbers involves understanding their properties, such as the commutative, associative, and distributive properties.
    • Number Sets: Understanding different sets of numbers, like natural numbers, whole numbers, integers, rational numbers, and irrational numbers.
    • Order of Operations: The correct sequence for solving expressions, often remembered by the acronym PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction).
  2. Simplifying Expressions:
    • Simplifying algebraic expressions by combining like terms, using distributive property, and applying exponent rules (e.g., product of powers, power of a power).
    • Factorization: Breaking down expressions into their simplest form, such as factoring polynomials or factoring quadratics.
  3. Equations and Inequalities:
    • Solving Linear Equations: Finding the value of the variable that makes the equation true (e.g., solving for x in 2x+3=7).
    • Solving Inequalities: Understanding how to solve and graph inequalities, such as 2x+3 < 7, and representing solutions on a number line.
    • Systems of Equations: Solving systems of linear equations using methods like substitution and elimination.
  4. Functions and Their Graphs:
    • Definition of a Function: A function is a relation that assigns exactly one output for every input.
    • Graphing Functions: Learn how to graph basic functions such as linear functions, quadratic functions, absolute value functions, and more.
    • Domain and Range: Understanding the set of possible input values (domain) and output values (range) of a function.
    • Transformations of Functions: How shifting, stretching, or reflecting the graph of a function can change its appearance.
  5. Polynomials:
    • Operations with Polynomials: Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing polynomials.
    • Factoring Polynomials: Factoring trinomials, difference of squares, and other types of polynomials.
    • Zeros of Polynomials: Finding the values of x that make a polynomial equal to zero , often through factoring or using the quadratic formula.
  6. Rational Expressions:
    • Simplifying Rational Expressions: Reducing fractions that contain polynomials in the numerator and denominator.
    • Operations with Rational Expressions: Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing rational expressions.
    • Solving Rational Equations: Solving equations where the variable appears in the numerator or denominator of a rational expression.
  7. Exponential and Logarithmic Functions:
    • Exponential Functions: Functions where the variable appears in the exponent (e.g.,f(x)=^a.
    • Logarithmic Functions: The inverse of exponential functions, and how to solve equations involving logarithms.
    • Laws of Logarithms: Understanding properties such as the product rule, quotient rule, and power rule for logarithms.
  8. Quadratic Equations:
    • Solving Quadratic Equations: Techniques like factoring, completing the square, and using the quadratic formula.
    • Graphing Parabolas: Understanding the graph of a quadratic function and its properties, such as the vertex, axis of symmetry, and x-intercepts.
  9. Sequences and Series:
    • Arithmetic Sequences: Sequences where the difference between consecutive terms is constant.
    • Geometric Sequences: Sequences where each term is multiplied by a constant factor.
    • Summation of Series: Using formulas to find the sum of a series (arithmetic or geometric).
  10. Applications of Algebra:
    • Word Problems: Translating real-world problems into algebraic equations and solving them.
    • Modeling Real-Life Situations: Using algebraic equations to model and solve problems in areas like finance (e.g., compound interest), physics (e.g., motion), and business (e.g., profit and cost).
